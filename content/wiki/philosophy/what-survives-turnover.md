# What survives turnover.

When your CFO leaves a traditional company, half the institutional knowledge of the financial architecture leaves with her.

In an Atrium company, that knowledge stays on the wiki — with citations, with reasoning, with decision context. The next CFO inherits a working brain, not an empty desk.

This is the most underestimated argument for Atrium until you have actually been through a senior-leader transition.

It applies symmetrically. CFO, COO, GC, Head of Product, the founding head of sales. Every senior departure today removes a substantial share of the company's institutional memory. The cost is invisible because it is baseline — paid every quarter, every board pack, every M&A diligence cycle, every regulator inquiry.

Atrium's compounding case is that this cost is not a fact of nature. It is a fact of architecture.

## What survives generations.

The same argument runs, with sharper teeth, across generations of a family enterprise.

When the founder of a family conglomerate dies or retires, the reasoning behind a half-century of decisions usually goes with him. Why the holding structure looks the way it does. Why the family exited textiles in the seventies and entered logistics in the nineties. Why a particular branch of the family controls the trust and another does not. Why this counsel, why that custodian, why a Singapore SFO and not Hong Kong.

In a traditional family, this reasoning lives in the founder's head, in the memory of one or two longest-tenured advisors, and in a notebook no one has read since 2003. The heirs inherit the assets. They do not inherit the reasoning.

In an Atrium family, the reasoning stays on the wiki — with citations, with the founder's own framings preserved in his own words, with the council resolutions versioned alongside the decisions they shaped.

The next generation inherits a working memory, not an empty desk.
